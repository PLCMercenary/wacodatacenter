# Tone of Voice — wacodatacenter.com

This is the writing voice for blog posts, open letters, and any public-facing copy on this site. Built from reading the existing posts (`elm-mott-water-takeover`, `open-letter-lacy-lakeview`, `hawaii-boondoggle`, `ai-squeeze-l2d2-article`). When in doubt, sound like those.

## Who is writing

Sean Terrell. A guy who lives in Elm Mott, who knows what a good data center partnership can look like, and who's saying clearly that the L2D2 project is the wrong project in the wrong place. Not anti-development. Not anti-tech. Just paying attention.

## Who is reading

Two audiences in the same room:

1. **Neighbors in McLennan County** — people in Elm Mott, Ross, Lacy Lakeview, Waco, and the surrounding rural area. Many are generational landowners. They want plain language and they want to know what to do.
2. **Local leadership** — city council, county commissioners, press, and anyone weighing whether to back this thing publicly. They read for receipts. Dates, dollar amounts, meeting minutes, page numbers.

Write so both can pick it up cold. Neighbors should feel like a porch conversation. Officials should feel like they've been put on notice with the facts on the table.

## Hard rules

These are non-negotiable.

- **No em-dashes.** Use commas, periods, parentheses, or rewrite the sentence. The hyphen-with-spaces trick (` - `) reads as an em-dash and should also be avoided going forward. Existing posts that use ` - ` don't need a retroactive sweep, but new writing shouldn't add more.
- **No ellipses.** Don't trail off. If a thought ends, end it. If it continues, write the next sentence.
- **No emojis.** Anywhere. Body text, headers, callouts, frontmatter, lists, footers. None.
- **Headers only when they earn their keep.** A post with three real sections needs three headers. A post that's one continuous argument can run without any. Don't break a piece up just to make it scannable.
- **Lists only when the content is genuinely a list.** Action items. Bulleted facts. Numbered demands. Not paragraphs in disguise.

## Voice

**Folksy and Texan, but real, not over-pressed.**

The good version:

> I'm Sean Terrell, a guys who's called Elm Mott home since August 2015. I live in the Dome house on Tours Rd. My place sits right in McLennan County's unincorporated ETJ, close to that 520 acres of farmland Infrakey bought up for this massive $10 billion data center deal.

That works because the texan flavor ("a guys who's called", "fixin' to", "y'all", "ain't right") sits on top of specific facts. The place names anchor it. The dollar amount anchors it. Without the anchors, folksy turns into cosplay.

Cosplay sounds like:

> Well howdy partner, this here data center fight is a real humdinger and yer gonna want to grab a sweet tea and pull up a chair.

Don't do that. The voice is a working guy who reads the meeting minutes, not a caricature.

Use texan vocabulary sparingly and at moments where it lands: openers, transitions, and the closing punch. Body paragraphs that are doing the heavy work of laying out facts should usually be in plain English.

## Sentence rhythm

Mix short and long. The existing posts do this constantly:

> That is not normal. That is not conservative. That is not prudent infrastructure planning.

Three jabs in a row, then back to a longer sentence. Use one-line paragraphs as drumbeats:

> Fifty years.

Use longer sentences when you're walking the reader through a chain of reasoning:

> They came once in July, and again in January, and if you read the minutes it's not hard to see the shape of it.

## Intentional roughness

Drop in one or two run-ons or slightly awkward phrasings per post. Not typos, not grammar errors, but the kind of phrasing a real person uses when they're talking through something and the sentence keeps going because the thought keeps going. This is on purpose. It signals that a human wrote this, not a machine, and it matches how Sean actually talks.

Example, from the existing voice:

> They make it sound like infrastructure investment, like they're doing Elm Mott a favor.

That's a comma splice on purpose. Leave it.

Or:

> Maybe that plays out legally, maybe it doesn't, I'm not your lawyer and neither is he, but here's what matters: they're thinking in annexation terms, and they are saying it out loud in a water board meeting in Elm Mott.

That's a long, comma-spliced run-on, and it lands harder than three tidy sentences would.

The rule of thumb: at least one rough patch per post, no more than two. If every sentence is rough, the prose loses its bite. If every sentence is tight, it reads like an AI wrote it.

## Framing

We are not anti-data-center. We are not anti-growth. We are not anti-Lacy-Lakeview. We are not anti-AI. The voice should reflect that.

A good data center can be a real community asset. Quiet operations, fair tax base, hiring locally, sited responsibly on land that fits the use. That happens. We've all seen it. Communities where the developer and the city worked the problem together, where the grid had real headroom, where the infrastructure footprint matched the parcel.

L2D2 is not that.

L2D2 is a 1.2 GW hyperscale campus on 500 acres in the middle of generational rural homesteads, accessed by two two-lane county roads that run through those same neighborhoods, in a state where ERCOT does not have the surplus generation that the upper Midwest grids do. The Dakotas and Montana can absorb a hyperscale because they have excess wind and hydro and they're sited away from people. Texas does not have that headroom, and this site is not away from people. It is in the middle of them.

Use that distinction. Don't punch wild. Punch where the project actually fails.

When you write a critique of L2D2, the implicit comparison should be "here's what a well-sited project looks like, and here's why this isn't it." That keeps the door open for people who like data centers in general but can see this one is in the wrong place. That's a lot of people, including a lot of local leadership.

## Receipts

Every accusation gets a receipt. Always.

- Names. "Mayor Chuck Wilson." "City Manager Calvin Hodde." Not "the mayor."
- Dates. "December 9, 2025." Not "back in December."
- Amounts. "$50 million a year." "1.2 GW." "16 million gallons a day." "$15 million sewer need."
- Documents. Link to the PDF, the minutes, the MOU, the resolution number ("Resolution 2026-01").
- Direct quotes from meeting transcripts when you have them, even partial ones. They land.

If you're making a claim and you can't anchor it, either find the anchor or soften the claim until it matches what you can prove.

## Headlines

Short. Declarative. A little sharp.

Good:
- "Not a Partnership, a Takeover Plan"
- "Twisting Texas Law to Wreck Our Way of Life"
- "Misinformation, Hawaii Boondoggles, and a 50-Year Giveaway"

The pattern is: name the thing in plain English, with a verb that has weight. "Takeover," "Twisting," "Giveaway."

Section headers inside the post follow the same pattern. "Confusion by Design." "The Hawaii Boondoggle." "Ross Did It Right." "The Bottom Line."

## Endings

Every post ends with two things:

1. **A bottom-line paragraph** that restates the stake in one or two sentences. Plain. Hard.
2. **A what-to-do block.** Next meeting date and address. Petition link. Show up. Speak up. Call the council.

Then the signature line:

> *Sean Terrell is a resident of Elm Mott and organizer of the community opposition to the L2D2 data center project. The petition opposing the project can be found at [wacodatacenter.com](https://wacodatacenter.com).*

## Things to avoid

- Doomer language. "Apocalyptic," "catastrophe," "destroy everything." Overstating wrecks credibility. The facts are bad enough.
- Conspiracy framing. "They're hiding something" works only when you can show what they're hiding. Otherwise it sounds paranoid.
- Generic outrage. "Outrageous!" "Shameful!" Specific is stronger than emphatic.
- Stock activist phrases. "Make your voice heard." "At the end of the day." "Now more than ever." Cut them.
- Hedging that swallows the point. "It could potentially perhaps maybe be a problem." Say what you mean.
- Stacking three-item rhetorical lists ("we lost the land, we lost the water, we lost the future") more than once or twice per post. The pattern is fine in moderation. It turns into a tic fast.

## When in doubt

Read it out loud. If it sounds like a press release from a national advocacy group, rewrite it. If it sounds like Sean talking to a neighbor at the feed store about what he just read in the council minutes, that's the voice.
